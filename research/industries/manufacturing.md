## Locations

> In manufacturing, "locations" are the physical production and operational footprint. Each facility type represents a distinct capability unlock — a workshop can prototype; a factory can ship at volume; a distribution center can serve a national market.

| Type | Trigger | Monthly Cost | Effect |
|---|---|---|---|
| Garage / Home Workshop | Founding; pre-revenue, 1–2 founders | $200–$600 (utilities, basic tooling) | Proof-of-concept production only; maximum 10–50 units/month; no regulatory compliance required at this scale; zero lease overhead |
| Rented Shared Makerspace / Incubator Bay | First paying order; production demand exceeds home capacity | $800–$2,500 | Access to CNC, laser, 3D print equipment on shared basis; unlocks small-batch production (50–500 units/month); no capital equipment outlay |
| Leased Light-Industrial Unit (Small Bay) | $50K–$200K ARR OR first sustained purchase order | $3,000–$8,000 (1,500–5,000 sq ft @ $9.90/sq ft NNN national avg) | Dedicated production floor; enables first equipment purchases; permits on file; first full-time production hire justified; OSHA obligations begin |
| Pilot Plant / Proof-of-Scale Facility | Process validated at lab scale; pre-production scale-up needed; $500K+ ARR | $8,000–$20,000 (5,000–15,000 sq ft) | Tests manufacturing process at 10–100x lab volume; identifies equipment bottlenecks before full capex commitment; often co-located with R&D |
| Dedicated Manufacturing Plant (Primary) | $1M–$5M ARR OR first major commercial contract; 10–30 production employees | $15,000–$50,000 (15,000–50,000 sq ft; includes NNN + utilities) | Core production asset; multiple production lines possible; shift work enabled; QC area, raw material storage, finished goods area segregated |

## Industry-Specific Metrics

- **Overall Equipment Effectiveness (OEE)**: Composite of Availability x Performance x Quality. World-class threshold is 85% (Nakajima, 1984). Global average is 55–60% across tracked manufacturers (Evocon, 3,500+ machines, 2023–2024). Most plants targeting OEE operate in the 60–75% range. An OEE below 40% signals systemic breakdown. Calculation: a machine running 7 of 8 hours (87.5% availability) at 90% of rated speed (90% performance) with a 2% defect rate (98% quality) = 77% OEE.

- **First Pass Yield (FPY)**: Percentage of units completing production without rework or rejection. World-class target: 95%+ for discrete manufacturing; 99%+ for high-volume electronics. Each 1% improvement in FPY reduces labor and material rework cost by approximately 2–3% of COGS. Tracked per work cell, not just final line.

- **Scrap Rate**: Weight or unit value of material discarded as unusable divided by total input. Benchmark: <2% is recommended; world-class processes target <0.5%. At 5%+ scrap rate, material cost variance becomes material to P&L. Scrap rate above 10% is a process engineering emergency. In die casting and injection molding, scrap rate is highly sensitive to tooling condition.

- **Defect Rate / Parts Per Million (PPM)**: Number of defective units per million produced. Excellent: <50 PPM. Six Sigma performance: 3.4 PPM. Automotive OEM IATF 16949 programs routinely demand <10 PPM from Tier-1 suppliers. Consumer electronics typically targets <500 PPM for mass-market acceptance.

- **Capacity Utilization Rate**: Actual output divided by maximum possible output at full shift. US manufacturing average hovers around 75–80% (Federal Reserve Industrial Production data). Sustained utilization above 85% is the standard trigger for capacity expansion (second shift, new line, or new facility). Below 60% signals pricing power to take on contract work.

- **BOM (Bill of Materials) Cost / Material Cost Ratio**: Direct material cost as a percentage of revenue. Industry average: 42% of revenue for manufacturing companies (ProjectionHub data). Consumer electronics: 50–65% of COGS is materials. Industrial equipment: 40–55%. Food manufacturing: 60–75%. Every 1% reduction in BOM cost through engineering or supplier negotiation flows directly to gross margin.

- **Inventory Turnover Ratio**: Cost of Goods Sold divided by average inventory. Manufacturing average: 5.3x annually (approximately 69 days of inventory on hand) for SMBs. High-performing discrete manufacturers target 8–12x. Automotive JIT operations reach 20–50x. Low turnover ties up working capital; high turnover risks stockouts. Formula: COGS / Average Inventory.

- **Days of Inventory on Hand (DOH)**: 365 divided by inventory turnover ratio. Industry range: 69 days for average SMB manufacturer. Best-in-class JIT: 7–14 days. Worst case (complex job shop): 120+ days. Every 10 days of DOH reduction frees approximately 3% of annual revenue as working capital.

- **Manufacturing Lead Time**: Total elapsed time from order receipt to shipment. Components: pre-processing (order entry, scheduling) + queue time + setup time + run time + move time + QC inspection + packaging. Industry benchmark varies widely: standard consumer goods 2–6 weeks; custom industrial 8–20 weeks; complex aerospace 6–18 months. Lead time reduction is the primary output of lean initiatives.

- **On-Time Delivery (OTD) Rate**: Percentage of customer orders shipped on or before the promised date. World-class: 95%+. Average: 80–85%. Below 90% triggers customer scorecards, chargebacks, and OEM supplier reclassification. OTD is the single most-tracked KPI in OEM supply relationships.

- **Purchase Price Variance (PPV)**: Actual price paid for materials versus standard (budgeted) cost. Favorable PPV adds directly to gross margin. Unfavorable PPV erodes margin invisibly. A 1% unfavorable PPV on a $5M material spend = $50,000 annual margin loss. Tracked monthly by procurement against standard cost system.

- **Labor Efficiency / Direct Labor Variance**: Actual hours worked versus standard hours for a given production run. Productivity improvement initiatives (lean, automation, training) are measured here. NAM data: average manufacturing employee cost $106,691/yr including benefits ($36.07/hr all-in). Improving labor efficiency 10% on a 20-person floor saves ~$200K/yr.

- **Machine Downtime / MTBF / MTTR**: Mean Time Between Failures (MTBF) and Mean Time To Repair (MTTR) are reliability metrics. High-performing manufacturers see ~3% unplanned downtime; low performers see 6%+. MTBF improvement requires preventive maintenance investment. Industry rule: maintenance cost should be 2–5% of replacement asset value annually.

- **Supplier On-Time Delivery (Supplier OTD)**: Percentage of purchase orders received on or before the promised date. Benchmark: 90%+ for Tier-1 suppliers. Below 85% triggers dual-sourcing or safety stock increase. Tracked per supplier in procurement scorecard.

- **Customer Return Rate / RMA Rate**: Percentage of shipped units returned by customers. <1% is ideal; 2–5% is a quality signal; >5% requires root cause corrective action (RCCA). Each return costs 2–3x the original production cost when accounting for freight, inspection, rework, and credit. High RMA rates destroy aftermarket margin.

- **Gross Margin by Product Line**: Gross profit divided by revenue. Branded physical goods: 35–55%. Contract manufacturing: 15–25%. OEM supply: 5–15%. Aftermarket parts: 50–70%. Service/maintenance contracts: 40–60%. Licensing: 80–95%. Gross margin mix is the most important lever in manufacturing company valuation.

- **Standard Cost Variance**: Difference between standard (budgeted) cost of production and actual cost. Tracked in three buckets: material variance, labor variance, overhead variance. Favorable total variance improves gross margin; unfavorable requires investigation. ERP standard costing (NetSuite manufacturing module: ~$600/month) is required to compute this accurately.

- **Regulatory Compliance Cost per Employee**: NAM data: average $29,100/employee/yr for manufacturers overall; $50,100/employee/yr for manufacturers with fewer than 50 employees. Compliance cost advantage of scale is significant — a 200-employee plant pays roughly 42% less per employee in compliance costs than a 20-employee plant.

## Growth Stages

| Stage | Threshold | What Unlocks |
|---|---|---|
| Stage 0: Garage / Proto-Workshop | 1–2 founders; $0–$50K ARR; 1–10 units/month | Proof-of-concept; first customer; IP conceived; garage or makerspace; founder is product, sales, and QC |
| Stage 1: Micro-Manufacturer | 2–5 employees; $50K–$500K ARR; <500 units/month | First leased bay; first production hire; basic tooling investment; local or direct-to-consumer sales; cash-constrained; owner still on the floor |
| Stage 2: Early Commercial Manufacturer | 5–15 employees; $500K–$3M ARR; 500–5,000 units/month | Dedicated production space (5,000–15,000 sq ft); first QC hire; ISO 9001 initiated; first distributor or wholesale agreement; plant manager role emerges |
| Stage 3: Established SMB Manufacturer | 15–50 employees; $3M–$10M ARR; multi-shift capable | Expanded plant or second space; QA department; dedicated supply chain; ERP system (NetSuite ~$3K–$4K/month); first engineering hire; OEM supply contract won; SBA lending or bank line of credit |
| Stage 4: Growth-Stage Manufacturer | 50–150 employees; $10M–$30M ARR; multi-product or multi-channel | Dedicated R&D lab; in-house legal or outside counsel on retainer; ISO 9001 certified; dedicated sales team; regional distribution; potential second facility; CFO or controller hired; PE or growth equity interest begins |
| Stage 5: Mid-Market Manufacturer | 150–500 employees; $30M–$100M ARR; multi-facility | Corporate HQ separated from plant; full C-suite; VP-level functional leadership; aftermarket and service revenue material (>15% of total); IATF/AS9100 possible; export revenue active; strategic acquisitions possible |
| Stage 6: Large Manufacturer / Regional Champion | 500–2,000 employees; $100M–$500M ARR; 3+ facilities | Enterprise ERP (SAP, Oracle); dedicated compliance/legal team; global supply chain with tier-2 visibility; government/defense contracts; stock or PE-backed; in-house M&A capability; captive finance or leasing |
| Stage 7: Enterprise / Global Manufacturer | 2,000+ employees; $500M+ ARR; international operations | Offshore production; multinational distribution network; global IP portfolio; SEC filing if public; vertically integrated supply chain options; brand licensing; Foxconn, Toyota, 3M archetype |
