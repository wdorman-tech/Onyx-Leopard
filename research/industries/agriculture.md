## Locations

> Locations in agriculture span owned and leased land, specialized production buildings, and support infrastructure. The trigger column reflects the USDA NASS farm typology thresholds (low-sales < $150K GCFI; moderate-sales $150K–$350K; midsize $350K–$999K; large $1M–$5M; very large > $5M) plus operational acreage and head-count milestones derived from extension budgets.

| Type | Trigger | Monthly Cost | Effect |
|---|---|---|---|
| Family Farmstead (Home Base) | Founding; owner-operator; sub-$150K GCFI | $800–$2,500 (property tax, utilities, basic maintenance) | Core home base; equipment storage in existing structures; no commercial facilities; production limited to what operator can physically manage alone; acreage ceiling ~200 acres solo |
| Leased Crop Acreage (Cash Rent) | First cash rent agreement; seeking scale beyond owned land; any GCFI tier | $200–$377/acre/month amortized annually (Corn Belt avg $242/acre; Piatt Co., IL peak $377/acre per NASS 2024) | Scales production acres without land purchase capital; Corn Belt average adds 200–500 acres per lease block; triggers operating loan need for input costs |

## Industry-Specific Metrics

- **Yield Per Acre**: Core productivity metric. Corn national average 177 bu/acre (NASS 2024); soybeans 51 bu/acre; winter wheat 50 bu/acre. Top-quartile operators achieve 200–230 bu/acre corn. Each additional bushel per acre on 1,000 acres at $4.50/bu = $4,500 additional gross revenue. Yield gap between top and bottom quartile operators commonly $30–$60/acre in profit.

- **Cost Per Bushel**: Total cost (operating + ownership) divided by yield. Breakeven: corn $5.27/bu (Purdue 2025); soybeans $12.30/bu (Purdue 2025); wheat approximately $6–$8/bu depending on yield. Operators below breakeven are cash-flow negative; marketing strategy (hedging, forward contracts) is required to lock margin above breakeven before price declines.

- **Cost Per Acre (Crop)**: Corn production 2024: ~$856/acre total; soybeans ~$613/acre; wheat ~$416/acre (USDA ERS/extension composite). Variable input split: seed $120–$180/acre, fertilizer $120–$200/acre, chemicals $60–$120/acre, drying/handling $20–$60/acre. Land cost (cash rent or opportunity cost) is the single largest or second-largest line item at $200–$377/acre in Corn Belt.

- **Land Value Per Acre**: Ranges from $3,000–$6,000 in lower-productivity regions to $8,000–$12,000 in prime Corn Belt counties (NASS 2024; Ohio avg $8,760, Wisconsin avg $6,120). Iowa prime cropland exceeded $14,000/acre in some 2024 sales. Appreciation has been 5–10%/year 2010–2024 in prime areas. Cash rent yield on purchase price: 2–4%.

- **Cash Rent Per Acre**: Corn Belt average $242/acre (NASS 2024); Illinois premium counties $350–$377/acre; Wisconsin $162/acre; Minnesota $200/acre. Rent represents 25–40% of total production cost per acre in high-rent counties. Cash rent negotiation is an annual financial leverage point for tenant operators.

- **Input Cost Ratio**: Input costs (seed, fertilizer, chemicals, fuel) as percent of gross revenue. Healthy row crop operation: 45–60% of revenue. Elevated corn price year (2022): ratio favorable; depressed corn price year (2024): ratio can exceed 75%, compressing margin. Fertilizer peaked 2022 (record), declined 2023–2024 but remains above 2018–2020 averages.

- **Commodity Price Exposure (Basis)**: Basis = local cash price minus nearby futures price. Corn basis ranges -$0.60 to +$0.10 depending on location, time of year, and elevator demand. Favorable basis management (storing, forward selling, using HTAs) can add $0.20–$0.50/bu to net price vs. harvest cash sale. Monitoring basis is a daily practice for sophisticated operators.

- **Crop Insurance Loss Ratio**: Ratio of indemnities paid to premiums earned. National average loss ratio for federal crop insurance is 0.80–1.10 depending on year and crop. Individual farm loss ratio below 1.0 means they received less than they paid; above 1.0 means the program paid out more than collected — typical in disaster years. Producers pay avg 38% of total premium; government subsidizes remainder.

- **Livestock Feed Conversion Ratio (FCR)**: Pounds of feed per pound of live weight gain. Benchmark FCRs: Broilers 1.8–2.0:1 (most efficient); Hogs/Swine 2.5–3.5:1; Backgrounding Cattle 5.0–7.0:1; Finishing Cattle 6.0–10.0:1. Feed cost is 50–80% of total livestock production cost, making FCR the primary driver of profitability. Each 0.1 improvement in FCR on 1,000 hogs saves approximately $2,000–$4,000 in feed cost.

- **Death Loss Rate**: Percent of livestock inventory lost to mortality. Targets: Cow-calf death loss <2% (cows), <5% (calves); Swine finishing death loss <3%; Poultry broiler condemnation rate <1%. Death loss above benchmark indicates management, biosecurity, or nutrition deficiency. 1% excess death loss on 1,000 finishing steers = ~$2,800 in lost revenue at 2024 cattle prices.

- **Seasonal Cash Flow Cycle**: Row crop farms have near-zero cash receipts January–August, massive outflow March–June (inputs, rent), and concentrated cash inflow September–December at harvest. Operating lines of credit bridge the gap; FSA operating loans up to $400K. A 1,000-acre corn-soy operation may draw $400K–$700K in operating credit from March–August and repay 90%+ at harvest. Working capital adequacy (current ratio >1.3) is the key financial health metric.

- **Machinery Cost Per Acre**: Well-managed large operations target <$110/acre for total machinery cost (Purdue benchmark). Operations >2,000 acres average $668/acre in machinery investment (farmdoc 2024). Custom farming vs. ownership breakeven typically at 500–1,500 acres depending on operation type. Precision ag upgrades add $50–$150/acre in investment but target $25–$80/acre in input savings through VR technology.

- **Gross Cash Farm Income (GCFI) Thresholds (USDA NASS 2022)**: Low-sales < $150,000 (74% of farms; 2% of sales); Moderate-sales $150K–$350K; Midsize $350K–$999K; Large $1M–$5M; Very large > $5M (105,384 farms = 6% of total farms but 76% of all agricultural product sales).

- **Agritourism Revenue per Farm**: Participating farms average $44,000 gross/year (USDA 2022 Census). Top performers (wedding barns, U-pick orchards, destination farms) exceed $200,000/year from agritourism alone. Only 1.5% of U.S. farms report agritourism income — significant headroom for adoption.

- **Carbon Sequestration Potential**: No-till and cover crop practices sequester approximately 0.1–0.5 tonnes CO2-eq per acre per year (protocols vary; additionality requirements strict). At $10–$35/tonne market rate, this equals $1–$17/acre/year in additional revenue — meaningful for large operations, marginal for small. Best positioned: large no-till operators already practicing conservation.

## Growth Stages

| Stage | USDA Threshold / Operational Profile | What Unlocks |
|---|---|---|
| Stage 1: Subsistence / Micro Farm | GCFI < $10,000; < 50 acres owned or leased; sole operator; direct sales only | Farmers market access; CSA with < 20 members; cottage food exemption (no processing facility required in most states); FSA microloan up to $50K; NRCS beginning farmer set-aside |
| Stage 2: Low-Sales Family Farm | GCFI $10K–$150K; 50–300 acres; owner-operator + 1 family member; first hired seasonal help | On-farm grain storage (first bin); operating line of credit; crop insurance enrollment; ARC/PLC program enrollment; EQIP cost-share; first equipment purchase financed; CSA up to 75 members |
| Stage 3: Moderate-Sales Farm | GCFI $150K–$350K; 300–750 acres; 1–2 full-time employees; first hired agronomist advisory | Precision ag equipment adoption (yield monitor, GPS); first livestock enterprise possible; cash rent expansion; FSA operating loan up to $400K; co-op input discount access; value-added product line launch |
| Stage 4: Midsize Family Farm | GCFI $350K–$999K; 750–2,500 acres; 3–6 employees; hired farm manager possible | Full precision ag stack (VR fertility, autosteering, drone scouting); dedicated grain merchandising strategy; first packing house or processing investment; organic transition possible; food broker relationship; carbon market enrollment; agritourism diversification |
| Stage 5: Large-Scale Family Farm | GCFI $1M–$5M; 2,500–8,000 acres; 8–20 employees; professional management team | Hired operations manager + agronomist on payroll; commercial cold storage; direct packer/processor contracts; SQF or GFSI certification for value-add line; multiple FSA farm numbers; land acquisition program; entity structuring (LLC, trust) for tax and estate; bank line of credit $1M+ |
| Stage 6: Very Large Farm / Multi-Farm Operation | GCFI > $5M; > 8,000 crop acres or equivalent livestock; 20–100 employees; professional C-suite | Corporate headquarters; acquisition of smaller operations; custom farming revenue stream; co-op board participation; commodity merchandising desk; institutional lender (not just community bank); ERP/farm management software enterprise license; investor or family office capital; potential vertical integration into processing |
| Stage 7: Vertically Integrated Agribusiness | GCFI > $20M; owns production, processing, and some distribution; brand in market | USDA-inspected processing plant with grant of inspection; cold chain logistics ownership; retail or food service direct accounts; brand marketing budget; national distribution via food broker network; GFSI-certified manufacturing; potential co-packing for other brands; ESG reporting and carbon credit program as revenue; trajectory toward Cargill/ADM model at processing scale or Driscoll's/Dole model at specialty crop scale |
